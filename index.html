About Burmese Python

Burmese Python, known as one of the five largest species of snakes in the world, which is native to South and Southeast Asia. They are mainly living in tropical rain forests, you may also find them near the water. Sometimes they also coil around trees.

Although Burmese Pythona are native to South Asia, it has become an invasive species in the United States in recent years, especially in South Florida, and has caused serious ecosystem problems.

On the contrary, the number of Burmese Python in the South and Southeast Asia keeps decreasing for the past few years and they have been listed as “Vulnerable” species by IUCN (International Union for Conservation of Nature).

Facing with this situation, what should we do to these big snakes? Kill or Help?

In South and Southeast Asia

In 2012, Burmese Pythons have been listed as “Vulnerable” species, which is not well-known to most people.

They are going through a localized decline which is greater than 80% over a ten-year period. According to the record from IUCN Red List, this snake is conservatively estimated to have declined by at least 30% over the past ten years across its global range as a result of over-harvesting for a variety of uses. To some extent this decline is compounded by the effects of habitat loss. The Burmese Pythons are therefore listed as Vulnerable.

The map on the left side shows the distribution range of the Burmese Python in South and Southeast Asia, which includes eastern India,Nepal,western Bhutan,southheastern Bangladesh, Myanmar, Thailand, Laos, Cambodia, Vietnam, northern continental Malaysia, far southern China (Fujian, Jiangxi, Guangdong, Hainan, Guangxi, and Yunnan),Hong Kong, and in Indonesia on Java, southern Sulawesi, Bali, and Sumbawa.(Source from wikipedia)

To animal merchants, Burmese Pythons have extremely high commercial values. For example, Burmese Python’s skin, meat and eggs can be sold for millions of dollars. Additional, Burmese Python’s Gallbladder and oil have high medical use value in South Asia, especially among herbalist doctors.

That’s why Burmese Pythons become so popular among hunters and merchants.

We can take Burmese Python’s skin as an example, the line chart on the right side shows the python skin’s trading numbers around the world between 2000 to 2014. At least 800 thousand meters of python skins were traded around the world every year according to these incomplete statistics from CITES (Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora).

Also, from raw skin to processed skin products, such as fashion coats, bags. The value can be increased several times and even more.

In South Florida

In contrast, in the United States, especially Florida, the Burmese Python has become an invasive species and caused serious problems. The most focused place in Florida is Miami-Dade county, with the Everglades National Park as a peak.

Because Burmese Python’s strong reproductive capacity(One big female Burmese Python can carry up to 100 eggs), the increasing speed of the pythons' number is very high. The National Park Service now puts the numbers there as high as 100,000.

In addition, because of its large size, adult Burmese Pythons have few predators, with alligators and humans being the exceptions. However, in some cases, the Burmese Python wins even in the battle with alligators. Sometimes alligators can become the food for the big snakes. When talking about the eating habits of the Burmese Python, studies show that they prey not only upon native mammal species, such as Norway rats, foxes and rabbits, they are also threatening other endangered species in Florida, for example, the endangered wood rats in Key Largo.

In its effort to prevent the continuous spread of non-native Burmese Pythons in the wild of the United States, the U.S Fish and Wildlife Service banned the “importation and interstate transportation” of the Burmese Python in March 2012.The rule specifically prohibited the interstate transport and importation of live snakes, their viable reproductive assets such as sperm and eggs, or any hybrids of the listed snake.

2016 Python Challenge
(Photo provided by Flickr)

Besides, aiming to engage more people into the action of removing pythons from Florida, the government started holding the “Python Challenge” in 2013. The challenge gave permitted hunters chances to hunt and kill pythons by themselves in several places in Florida. According to the length, amount and weight, hunters are awarded different amount of money. In the 2016 Python Challenge, the winning team lead by Bill Booth hunted for 33 Burmese Pythons during the game and gained 5000 dollars. At the same time, they also hunted for the longest Burmese Python: 15 feet and 125 pounds, which made them won another 3000 dollars.

However, the regulation of the challenge don't prevent the hunters from killing pythons. During the game, bolt guns and fire arms are suggested to be used while hunting, and hunters usually just kill the pythons without thinking, instead of keeping them alive. This makes it much easier for them to get a big snake.
Some animal rights groups have blasted the event for the unethical way the snakes are killed.

  • Leo Sanchez:
    Burmese Python Removement Volunteer

    • Instead of defining him as a Burmese Python hunter, I prefer to call him a python removement volunteer or a python lover. He never hunts Burmese Python to exchange their skins or meat for money, actually he has barely achieved benefits from the Burmese Pythons he hunted. He volunteers to remove Burmese Pythons just for fun and wants to protect the ecosystem of the Everglades.

    Python Removement Volunteer: Leo Sanchez
    (Photo taken by Zhou Fang)

    “I will never kill one python, I love and respect them,” Leo kept saying this in the day I spent with him. He also said he usually used his bare hands instead of any other tools which would hurt the pythons.

    Every time Leo got a Burmese Python, he had to donate it to universities or other research centers because he has no permission from Florida Fish and Wildlife Conservation Commission to keep Burmese Pythons legally. (Since 2012, it became illegal to own a Burmese Python in Florida after the Fish and Wildlife Conservation Commission banned importation and interstate transportation of Burmese Pythons inside Florida) “I don't want to do this because I know they (the Burmese Python) will be dead there,” he said. “But usually the University will use humanistic ways to put them down, for example I know, they will use gas to kill them without pain.”

    There is no doubt that Leo has lots of knowledge about Burmese Pythons, which is different from scientists gaining knowledge from researching, Leo learned from hundreds of trips he had in the Everglades. “Actually pythons usually won’t live along the water, water is just one place they can hide and escape when they are being traced,” Leo also told me temperature and weather are the keys to finding Burmese Pythons and other snakes. Early morning and evening with some sun are the best chances because pythons, which are cold blooded animals, need sunshine to keep their life. Additional, the stone crack is their favorite home, it will be better if there is a water resource nearby. You will have bigger chances to see snakes across the road if there are rocks and streams on the two sides of the road.

    Research has confirmed that Burmese Pythons are responsible for the rapidly decrease of the number of small mammals in the Everglades, which includes a lot of endangered species, for example, wood rats.

    Although Leo is a big fan of the Burmese Python, he has never denied the huge threat to the ecosystem of the Everglades given by the Burmese Python. “They must be removed, otherwise the mammals will disappear in the future, they eat everything, even alligators,” but Leo doesn’t agree that killing them is the only solution. “They are pythons, they are just doing things by their instincts and this is not their fault. I wish there is a way we can remove them from Florida to their native area, which is Asia, without hurting them.”

    Leo was bitten by a python on chest
    (Photo provided by Leo Sanchez)

    As a python removement volunteer, Leo got permission from Fish and Wildlife Conservation Commission which allows him to hunt Burmese Pythons along the edge of the Everglades. However, Leo knows deeply that in the shallows of the center areas of the Everglades, there are bigger Burmese Pythons breeding. Only hurting small pythons along the edge has limited effect on controlling the number of Burmese Pythons. Burmese Pythons have a striking reproductive capability. A large female Burmese Python can carry up to one hundred eggs in its body. From Leo’s opinion, the government should give people like him more access to the center of the Everglades. Once they can walk deeper into the center shallows, there will be more chances to remove those “big guys”.

  • Joe Wasilewski:
    Wildlife Biologist

    • Joe Wasilewski’s backyard is just like a mini-sized park. There is a large cage in the middle of it, inside of which lives various kinds of reptiles and a dinosaur model is standing just beside the cage. Anybody comeing in this backyard can be sure of what kind home owner lives here --- a reptile lover.

    Joe is both a wildlife biologist and a big lover of reptiles. He has been involved in Burmese Pythons research since the first time they were discovered in the Everglades. He and one of his colleagues found the first hatching Burmese Python and knew they were actually breeding there.

    Joe with his Burmese Python (Photo taken by Zhou Fang)

    Recently, Joe is doing research about Radiotelemetry and Control of Burmese Python. Joe introduced this in order to understand the extent of the pythons' impact on Florida’s ecosystems and created methods to control the population. They are using dogs, radiotelemetry and other technology (GPS transmitters, accelerometers) to learn what habitats Burmese Pythons have, how far they move and where their breeding groups live. Usually they will put the sensor into the body of the caught Burmese Pythons and track them. They can find out what kinds of animals they love to eat which can help them determine what animals are in risk. “We have found rats, including endangered wood rats, rabbits, even deer inside their bodies,” Joe added that they can also learn what temperature and weather they like, what kind of environment they love to stay in, which can help find methods to locate them and control their number.

    Joe doesn’t think that transporting Burmese Pythons back to Asia, where they come from, can be a way to control their number. “Scientifically, logistically, legally, it’s not going to happen at this time,” Joe explained. Legally, the Fish and Wildlife Conversation Commission banned the importation and interstate transportation of Burmese Pythons inside Florida. From biology, once Burmese Pythons have lived in Florida, their bodies will carry message that indicates they are resistant to Florida and they will bring bad impacts on the Asia ecosystem if they are brought back. For now, the only way is to use humanized way to remove them.

    To the opinion that it was the pet owners’ release of Burmese Pythons that led the out of control of these big killers, Joe said it was true that pet owners are releasing pythons, but the main reason of the invasion was due to the Andrew Hurricane in 1992. There was an importer of wildlife just next to the Everglades and there were 900 pythons were blown to the Everglades. Related researches also show that the number of Burmese Pythons strikes dramatically after 2000.

    Wildlife Biologist: Joe Wasilewski
    Photo taken by Zhou Fang

    As a legal Burmese Python owner, Joe has two Burmese Pythons at home. “I don’t consider they are my pets, they are my employees,” Joe always jokes. In fact, besides research works, Joe also attends television productions and educational shows with his Burmese Pythons. For example, he just finished “Reptile Show” in Jungle Island of Zoo Miami. “People should do research before they decide to have any kinds of pets,” he said. “They should learn about how big they will grow up to and what kind of food they need.”

    Joe said the main reason why pet owners release Burmese Pythons into wild is that they underestimated the growth rate and attack ability of the pythons. And some of them just keep feeding Burmese Pythons which leads them grow too big for people to handle. That’s why he wants to do these shows, not only sharing his researches but telling people how to raise their reptile pets correctly.
